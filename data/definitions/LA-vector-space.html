A vector space over a field \(k\) is a \(k\)-module. Its elements are called vectors. Concretely, a vector space over \(k\) is a set \(V\) with a distinguished object \(0 \in V\) and two operations \[ + : V \times V \to V, \quad \cdot : k \times V \to V \] satisfying
  • (commutativity) \(x + y = y + x\) for all \(x, y \in V\),
  • (associativity) \((x + y) + z = x + (y + z)\) for all \(x, y, z \in V\),
  • (zero vector) \(x + 0 = x = 0 + x\) for all \(x \in V\),
  • (negatives) for all \(x \in V\) there exists some \(-x \in V\) such that \(x + (-x) = 0\),
  • (associativity) \(\lambda \cdot (\mu \cdot x) = (\lambda \mu) \cdot x\) for all \(x \in V\) and \(\lambda, \mu \in k\),
  • (unit) \(1 \cdot x = x\) for all \(x \in V\),
  • (distributivity 1) \(\lambda \cdot (x + y) = (\lambda \cdot x) + (\lambda \cdot y)\) for all \(x, y \in V\) and \(\lambda \in k\),
  • (distributivity 2) \((\lambda + \mu) \cdot x = (\lambda \cdot x) + (\mu \cdot x)\) for all \(x \in V\) and \(\lambda, \mu \in k\).
