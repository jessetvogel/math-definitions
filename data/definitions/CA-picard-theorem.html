Picard’s little theorem states that, for any non-constant entire function \(f : \CC \to \CC\), the set of values \(f\) attains is either the whole of \(\CC\) or \(\CC\) minus one point.
Picard’s great theorem states that, for any analytic function \(f : \CC \to \CC\) that has an essential singularity at a point \(z\), the function \(f\) attains, on any punctured neighborhood of \(z\), all possible complex values, with at most one exception, infinitely often.
