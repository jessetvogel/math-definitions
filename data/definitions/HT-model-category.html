A model category is a category \(\mathcal{C}\) with three distinguished classes of maps: weak equivalences, fibrations and cofibrations, each of which is closed under composition and contains all identity maps. A map which is both a fibration (resp. cofibration) and a weak equivalence is called an acyclic fibration (resp. acyclic cofibration). The following axioms are required.
  • Finite limits and colimits exist in \(\mathcal{C}\).
  • For maps \(f\) and \(g\) in \(\mathcal{C}\) such that \(gf\) is defined, if two out of the three maps \(f, g\) and \(gf\) are weak equivalences, then so is the third.
  • If \(f\) is a retract of \(g\), and \(g\) is a fibration, cofibration or a weak equivalence, then so is \(f\).
  • For any commutative diagram such that either (i) \(i\) is a cofibration and \(p\) an acyclic fibration, or (ii) \(i\) an acyclic cofibration and \(p\) a fibration, there exists a lift \(h : B \to X\) making the diagram commute.
  • Any map \(f\) can be factored in two ways: (i) \(f = pi\) with \(i\) a cofibration and \(p\) an acyclic fibration, and (ii) \(f = pi\) with \(i\) an acyclic cofibration and \(p\) a fibration.
By the first axiom, \(\mathcal{C}\) has an initial object \(\varnothing\) and a terminal object \(\star\). An object \(A\) of \(\mathcal{C}\) is called cofibrant if \(\varnothing \to A\) is a cofibration, and fibrant if \(A \to \star\) is a fibration.
