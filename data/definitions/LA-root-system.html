Let \(V\) be a finite-dimensional real or complex vector space with an inner product \(\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle\). A root system \(\Phi\) in \(V\) is a finite set of non-zero vectors in \(V\), called roots, such that
  • (span) \(\Phi\) spans \(V\),
  • (multiples) if \(\alpha \in \Phi\), then \(n \alpha \in \Phi \iff n = \pm 1\),
  • (reflection) \(\beta - \frac{2 \langle \alpha, \beta \rangle}{\langle \alpha, \alpha \rangle} \alpha \in \Phi\) for all \(\alpha, \beta \in \Phi\),
  • (integrality) \(\frac{2 \langle \alpha, \beta \rangle}{\langle \alpha, \alpha \rangle}\) is an integer for all \(\alpha, \beta \in \Phi\).
Given a root system \(\Phi\), one can always choose (non-uniquely) a subset \(\Phi^+ \subset \Phi\) such that
  • for all \(\alpha \in \Phi\), either \(\alpha \in \Phi^+\) or \(-\alpha \in \Phi^+\), but not both,
  • for distinct \(\alpha, \beta \in \Phi^+\) such that \(\alpha + \beta \in \Phi\), also \(\alpha + \beta \in \Phi^+\).
Roots in \(\Phi^+\) are called positive and roots in \(-\Phi^+\) are called negative. An root in \(\Phi^+\) is called simple if it cannot be written as the sum of two positive roots. The set \(\Delta\) of simple roots has the property that every \(\alpha \in \Phi\) is a linear combination of elements of \(\Delta\) with integer coefficients, which are either all positive or all negative.
