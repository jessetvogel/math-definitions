Let \(\mathcal{A}\) be an abelian category. The homotopy category \(\textbf{K}(\mathcal{A})\) is defined to be the category whose objects are chain complexes in \(\mathcal{A}\), and morphisms are chain maps modulo homotopy.
The derived category \(\textbf{D}(\mathcal{A})\) is obtained from \(\textbf{K}(\mathcal{A})\) by ‘pretending’ any quasi-isomorphism is an isomorphism. Concretely, a morphism from \(A^\bdot\) to \(B^\bdot\) is given by a roof where \(f\) is a quasi-isomorphism, and we think of this roof as \(g \circ f^{-1}\) (even though \(f^{-1}\) might not exist). Technically, one localizes the category \(\textbf{K}(\mathcal{A})\) w.r.t. quasi-isomorphisms.
Both \(\textbf{K}(\mathcal{A})\) and \(\textbf{D}(\mathcal{A})\) naturally admit the structure of a triangulated category. In both cases, a triangle is distinguished if it is isomorphic to one of the form \[ A^\bdot \xrightarrow{f} B^\bdot \to C(f) \xrightarrow{\pi} A^\bdot[1] , \] where \(C(f)\) is the mapping cone of \(f\). That is, \[ C(f)^i = A^{i + 1} \oplus B^i \quad \text{and} \quad d_{C(f)}^i = \begin{pmatrix} -d_A^{i + 1} & 0 \\ f^{i + 1} & d_B^i \end{pmatrix} . \]
