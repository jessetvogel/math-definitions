Let \(\mathcal{A}\) be an abelian category. The derived category \(\textbf{D}(\mathcal{A})\) is obtained from the homotopy category \(\textbf{K}(\mathcal{A})\) by localizing with respect to quasi-isomorphisms. Concretely, a morphism from \(A^\bdot\) to \(B^\bdot\) is given by a roof where \(f\) is a quasi-isomorphism, and we think of this roof as \(g \circ f^{-1}\) (even though \(f^{-1}\) might not exist).
The derived category \(\textbf{D}(\mathcal{A})\) naturally admits the structure of a triangulated category: a triangle is distinguished if it is isomorphic to one of the form \[ A^\bdot \xrightarrow{f} B^\bdot \to C(f) \xrightarrow{\pi} A^\bdot[1] , \] where \(C(f)\) is the mapping cone of \(f\).
