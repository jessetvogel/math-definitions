Let \(R\) be a commutative ring. A polynomial \(f \in R[x_1, \ldots, x_n]\) is symmetric if it is invariant under any permutation of the variables, that is, \[ f(x_{\sigma(1)}, \ldots, x_{\sigma(n)}) = f(x_1, \ldots, x_n) \quad \textup{ for all } \sigma \in S_n . \] For any \(0 \le k \le n\), the \(k\)th elementary symmetric polynomial is given by \[ e_k = \sum_{1 \le i_1 < \ldots < i_k \le n} x_{i_1} \cdots x_{i_k} . \] The fundamental theorem of symmetric polynomials states that any symmetric polynomial \(f \in R[x_1, \ldots, x_n]\) can be written uniquely as a polynomial in the elementary symmetric polynomials \(e_k\). In other words, there is an isomorphism \(R[x_1, \ldots, x_n]^{S_n} \simeq R[e_1, \ldots, e_n]\).
