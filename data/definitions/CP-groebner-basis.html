Let \(I \subset k[x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n]\) be an ideal. A generating set \(G = \{ g_1, g_2, \ldots, g_n \}\) for \(I\) is called a Gröbner basis for \(I\) if for all non-zero \(f \in I\), the leading monomial \(\textup{lm}(g_i)\) divides \(\textup{lm}(f)\) for some \(i\).
A Gröbner basis \(G\) is called minimal if the leading coefficients \(\text{lc}(g_i) = 1\) for all \(i\), and the leading monomial \(\textup{lm}(g_i)\) does not divide \(\textup{lm}(g_j)\) for \(i \ne j\).
A Gröbner basis \(G\) is called reduced if the leading coefficients \(\text{lc}(g_i) = 1\) for all \(i\), and \(g_i\) is reduced with respect to \(G - \{ g_i \}\). A reduced Gröbner basis for \(I\) always exists and is unique.
