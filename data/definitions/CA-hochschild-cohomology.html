Let \(k\) be a field, \(R\) an \(k\)-algebra, and \(M\) a bimodule over \(R\). Let \(C_n(R, M) := M \otimes R^{\otimes n}\), and define boundary maps \(d_{n, i} : C_n(R, M) \to C_{n - 1}(R, M)\) for \(0 \le i \le n\) by \[ \begin{aligned} d_{n, 0}(r \otimes m_1 \otimes \cdots \otimes m_n) &= mr_1 \otimes r_2 \otimes \cdots \otimes r_n , \\ d_{n, i}(r \otimes m_1 \otimes \cdots \otimes m_n) &= m \otimes r_1 \otimes \cdots \otimes r_i r_{i + 1} \otimes \cdots \otimes r_n , \\ d_{n, n}(r \otimes m_1 \otimes \cdots \otimes m_n) &= r_n m \otimes r_2 \otimes \cdots \otimes r_{n - 1} . \end{aligned} \] Then \(C_n(R, M)\) with differential \(d_n = \sum_{i = 0}^{n} = (-i)^i d_{n, i}\) is a chain complex called the Hochschild complex. \[ \cdots \xrightarrow{d} M \otimes R^{\otimes n} \xrightarrow{d} M \otimes R^{\otimes n - 1} \xrightarrow{d} \cdots \xrightarrow{d} M \otimes R \xrightarrow{d} M \to 0 \] The corresponding homology \(HH_i(R, M)\) is called the Hochschild homology of \(R\) with coefficients in \(M\).
Similarly, Hochschild cohomology is defined by from the cocomplex \[ 0 \to M \xrightarrow{\partial} \Hom_k(R, M) \xrightarrow{\partial} \cdots \xrightarrow{\partial} \Hom_k(R^{\otimes n}, M) \xrightarrow{\partial} \Hom_k(R^{\otimes n + 1}, M) \xrightarrow{\partial} \cdots \] where \(\partial^n = \sum_{i = 0}^n (-1)^i \partial^{n, i}\) is given by \[ \begin{aligned} (\partial^{n, 0} f)(r_1 \otimes \cdots \otimes r_n) &= r_1 f(r_2 \otimes \cdots \otimes r_n) , \\ (\partial^{n, 1} f)(r_1 \otimes \cdots \otimes r_n) &= f(r_1 \otimes \cdots \otimes r_i r_{i + 1} \otimes \cdots \otimes r_n) , \\ (\partial^{n, n} f)(r_1 \otimes \cdots \otimes r_n) &= f(r_1 \otimes \cdots \otimes r_{n - 1}) r_n . \end{aligned} \] The corresponding cohomology \(HH^n(R, M)\) is the Hochschild cohomology of \(R\) with coefficients in \(M\).
