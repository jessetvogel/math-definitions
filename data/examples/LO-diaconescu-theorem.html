Let \(P\) be any proposition. Define the sets \[ A = \{ x \in \{ 0, 1 \} \mid (x = 0) \lor P \} \quad \textup{ and } \quad B = \{ x \in \{ 0, 1 \} \mid (x = 1) \lor P \} . \] By the axiom of choice, there exists a function \(f : \{ A, B \} \to A \cup B\) such that \(f(A) \in A\) and \(f(B) \in B\). By definition of \(A\) and \(B\), this means that \[ (f(A) = 0) \lor P \quad \textup{ and } \quad (f(B) = 1) \lor P , \] which is equivalent to \[ (f(A) \ne f(B)) \lor P . \] Now, since \(P \Rightarrow (A = B) \Rightarrow (f(A) = f(B))\), it follows that \((f(A) \ne f(B)) \Rightarrow \neg P\), which shows that \(\neg P \lor P\).
