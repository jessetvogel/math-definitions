Let \(X\) be the suspension of two disjoint circles (think of two bananas whose ends are connected). The singular homology of \(X\) is given by \[ H_0(X; \QQ) = \QQ, \quad H_1(X; \QQ) = \QQ, \quad H_2(X; \QQ) = \QQ \oplus \QQ . \] Indeed, \(X\) has one connected component, \(H_1(X; \QQ)\) is generated by the loop along both bananas, and \(H_2(X; \QQ)\) is generated by the \(2\)-chains given by the bananas.
In contrast, let us consider the intersection homology of \(X = X_2 \supset X_1 = X_0 = \{ a, b \}\), where \(a\) and \(b\) are the points connecting the two bananas. Since we only need \(p(2) = 0\), the choice of perversity does not matter. The \(p\)-allowable singular \(0\)-simplices are those points that are not \(a\) and \(b\). The \(p\)-allowable singular \(1\)-simplices are those that do not hit \(a\) and \(b\). The \(p\)-allowable singular \(2\)-simplices are those \(\sigma : \Delta^2 \to X\) such that \(\sigma^{-1}(\{ a, b \})\) only contains the vertices of \(\Delta^2\). Therefore, one finds \[ I^p H_0(X; \QQ) = \QQ \oplus \QQ, \quad I^p H_1(X; \QQ) = 0, \quad I^p H_2(X; \QQ) = \QQ \oplus \QQ \] where \(I^p H_0(X; \QQ)\) is generated by a point on each banana, and \(I^p H_2(X; \QQ)\) is the same as \(H_2(X; \QQ)\). Note that the singular \(1\)-chain which goes along both bananas is no longer allowed. Also, two points on different bananas cannot be connected by an (allowable) \(1\)-chain anymore.
