• The polynomial ring \(R = k[x_1, \ldots, x_n]\) is a Koszul algebra over \(k\), as a desired resolution is given by the Koszul complex \[ 0 \to R(-n) \to \cdots \to R(-i)^{\binom{n}{i}} \to \cdots \to R(-1)^n \to R \to k \to 0 . \]
  • The ring \(R = k[x, y]/(xy)\) is a Koszul algebra over \(k\), even though a resolution of \(k\) \[ \cdots \to R^2 \xrightarrow{\begin{pmatrix} y & 0 \\ 0 & x \end{pmatrix}} R^2 \xrightarrow{\begin{pmatrix} x & 0 \\ 0 & y \end{pmatrix}} R^2 \xrightarrow{\begin{pmatrix} y & 0 \\ 0 & x \end{pmatrix}} R^2 \xrightarrow{\begin{pmatrix} x & y \end{pmatrix}} R \to k \to 0 \] has infinite length.
  • The ring \(R = k[x] / (x^3)\) is not a Koszul algebra over \(k\). Namely, when trying to construct a resolution, we obtain \[ R \xrightarrow{x^2 \cdot} R \xrightarrow{x \cdot} R \to k \to 0 , \] but \(x^2\) is not a linear term.
